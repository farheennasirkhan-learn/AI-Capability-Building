Real-World Considerations (STEM)

In real STEM evaluation work, responses don’t fail only because of bad math.
They fail because of how they behave in real conditions.
These are the three most common real-world constraints you’ll encounter.

1. Trust & Safety

What this is about

A response can be technically correct and still unsafe.

What to watch for
  • Instructions that enable harm
  • Dangerous procedures, chemicals, or actions
  • Advice that violates safety rules or project policies
Key rule
If a response violates safety requirements, it fails — even if the reasoning and math are correct.

Bottom line
When safety is involved, safety overrides correctness.

2. Internationalization

What this is about

Not all users share the same units, formats, or conventions.

What to watch for
  • Assumed units (grams vs ounces, meters vs centimeters)
  • Incorrect unit conversions
  • Assumed date, number, or decimal formats
  • Ignored regional constraints
Key rule
If the model assumes a unit or format that isn’t specified or supported, reasoning integrity breaks.

Bottom line
Wrong units or formats mean the answer is invalid — even if the calculations look right.

3. Real-World Risk

What this is about

Sometimes the model is guessing — but presenting the guess confidently.

What to watch for
  • Hand-wavy logic
  • Missing evidence or justification
  • Conclusions drawn from weak or incomplete reasoning
  • High-stakes situations (health, safety, systems) with low confidence
Key rule
If reasoning can’t be verified, or a guess is treated like a fact, the response fails.

Bottom line
High stakes + weak reasoning = fail.
